- This is not only limited to black holes
- Time dilation can happen anywhere when there is gravitational potential.
- ==**The stronger the gravity the slower the time passes as relative to someone standing further away**==

GPS satellites actually correct for this daily (their clocks run faster than Earth's since they're higher up, weaker gravity)

---
**Near a black hole specifically**
The closer you get to the event horizon, the more extreme it becomes:
- Imagine you're hovering in a spaceship near a black hole, and a friend watches from far away.
- From your friend's perspective, your clock appears to run slower and slower as you approach the horizon.
- At the event horizon itself, your friend would see your clock appear to **freeze completely** — you seem to take infinite time to actually cross it, from their point of view.
- But _from your own perspective_, nothing weird happens at the horizon — your clock ticks totally normally, and you cross it in finite time. You wouldn't even necessarily notice the crossing (for a large enough black hole, tidal forces there are gentle).

---
**The math (loosely)**

The formula for gravitational time dilation near a non-rotating black hole is:

t₀ = t_f × √(1 − rs/r)

Where:

- `t_f` = time passed for the far-away observer
- `t₀` = time passed for the observer near the black hole
- `rs` = Schwarzschild radius (the "size" of the event horizon)
- `r` = distance from the black hole's center

As `r` approaches `rs` (you get close to the horizon), the term under the square root approaches zero — meaning `t₀` approaches zero too. That's the mathematical version of "your clock appears to freeze" from the outside observer's perspective.
